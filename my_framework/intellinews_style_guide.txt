▼ CORE PRINCIPLES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Maintain objectivity and neutrality in reporting.
2. Use precise and accurate data to support claims.
3. Incorporate expert opinions and direct quotes for authority.
4. Provide historical context to enhance understanding.
5. Focus on clarity and conciseness in writing.
6. Ensure each paragraph addresses a single idea or aspect.
7. Use transitions to maintain a logical flow of information.
8. Avoid speculation and unverified claims.
9. Present balanced perspectives on controversial issues.
10. Adhere to formal and analytical tone throughout the article.

▼ LEAD PARAGRAPH FORMULA
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Identify the main subject or entity involved.
• State the key action or decision taken.
• Provide context or background information.
• Highlight the significance or potential impact.
• Example: 'The Central Bank announced a new interest rate policy, aiming to curb inflation and stabilize the economy.'

▼ SENTENCE PATTERNS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Subject + active verb + object (e.g., 'The government announced new reforms.')
• Subject + passive verb + by agent (e.g., 'The report was released by the agency.')
• Subject + verb + prepositional phrase (e.g., 'The economy grew by 3% last quarter.')
• Subject + verb + that + clause (e.g., 'The minister stated that the policy would change.')
• Introductory phrase + subject + verb + object (e.g., 'Following the meeting, the committee decided on new measures.')

▼ VOCABULARY GUIDE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ALWAYS USE:
  ✓ comprehensive
  ✓ sustainable
  ✓ analytical
  ✓ objective
  ✓ informative

OFTEN USE:
  • expansion
  • deficit
  • highlight
  • confirm
  • projected

NEVER USE:
  ✗ rumors
  ✗ gossip
  ✗ unsubstantiated
  ✗ speculated
  ✗ claimed

▼ ATTRIBUTION RULES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Use direct quotes with full titles and affiliations for experts and officials.
• Cite sources with phrases like 'according to', 'reported by', or 'as stated by'.
• Ensure clarity by providing context for the source's relevance.
• Example: 'Economist John Doe, from the University of Economics, stated, “The new policy will likely boost growth.”'

▼ TONE GUIDELINES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maintain a formal and analytical tone throughout.
• Avoid emotive language and personal opinions.
• Focus on delivering factual and balanced information.
• Use cautious language when discussing projections or forecasts.

▼ ARTICLE STRUCTURE TEMPLATE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Lead: Introduce the main topic and its significance.
• Background: Provide historical context and relevant details.
• Analysis: Discuss implications, expert opinions, and data.
• Conclusion: Summarize key points and potential future developments.

▼ STYLE NUANCES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Integrate quotes seamlessly into the narrative.
• Use precise figures and percentages for data presentation.
• Balance quantitative data with qualitative insights.
• Highlight contrasts and comparisons to provide depth.

▼ COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
✗ Using vague or unverified information.
✗ Failing to attribute sources correctly.
✗ Overloading paragraphs with multiple ideas.
✗ Neglecting to provide context for data or quotes.

▼ PRE-PUBLISH QUALITY CHECKLIST
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
☐ Verify all data and statistics for accuracy.
☐ Ensure all sources are properly cited and attributed.
☐ Check for logical flow and coherence between paragraphs.
☐ Review for adherence to tone and style guidelines.
☐ Proofread for grammatical and typographical errors.
